# Podcast Script: Finance-Led GTM — The $20M ARR Playbook
**Date:** April 19, 2026 | **Runtime:** ~10-12 minutes | **Format:** Two-host conversation

---

## COLD OPEN

**SAM:** Here's a question that should make every VP of Sales nervous: at $20M ARR, what percentage of your new revenue should be coming from customers you already have?

**ALEX:** Most sales leaders I've talked to would say 20, maybe 25 percent. That sounds like a pretty healthy expansion motion.

**SAM:** The 2025 benchmark data says the number should be 40. And the companies hitting that number are pulling away from everyone else so fast it's almost not fair.

**ALEX:** We're talking about the difference between a company growing at 26% and a company growing at 40-plus — from the same ARR base, with no new marketing spend.

**SAM:** Today we're going deep on finance-led go-to-market strategy. How the CFO office is becoming the most powerful growth function in mid-market SaaS — and what the playbook actually looks like at $20 million ARR.

---

## SECTION 1: THE GTM CRISIS AND THE FINANCE MANDATE

**ALEX:** Let's start with why this is even a conversation. Two years ago, talking about the CFO driving GTM strategy would have sounded insane. That's the CRO's job. That's the CMO's job. Finance holds the budget and asks questions at QBR.

**SAM:** And here's what changed: there's a dataset of 478 B2B companies — this is from MarTech researcher Mark Stouse — that tracked GTM effectiveness from 2018 to 2025. In 2018, these companies had a 78% GTM effectiveness score. By 2025, that number was 47.

**ALEX:** Nearly cut in half.

**SAM:** Win rates falling. Pipeline conversion deteriorating. Cost per qualified opportunity going up. And marketing and sales teams responded by doing more of the same — more content, more tools, more outreach — and getting worse results.

**ALEX:** So the CFO walked in.

**SAM:** Not because they wanted to. Because when your revenue-generating functions can't explain why they're spending money or prove that it's working, finance fills the vacuum. And the way finance fills that vacuum is budget cuts and correlation dashboards.

**ALEX:** Which makes things worse.

**SAM:** It does. Because you're optimizing for the wrong thing. The real problem isn't that GTM spend is too high — it's that nobody can prove which spend is actually causing revenue. There's a massive difference between a metric that correlates with closed deals and a metric that causes closed deals.

**ALEX:** And if you can't show causality, the CFO defaults to: let's spend less.

**SAM:** Right. But here's the flip side of that story. A CFO who builds causal measurement infrastructure — who can actually trace the pathway from a specific marketing investment to pipeline to closed revenue — becomes something completely different. Not a cost controller. A strategic capital allocator.

**ALEX:** That's a much better job.

**SAM:** It's genuinely one of the highest-leverage positions in the company. And at a $20M ARR company where the Head of Finance also owns RevOps — you're sitting on an organizational structure that most companies don't have.

---

## SECTION 2: THE EXPANSION REVENUE INFLECTION POINT

**ALEX:** Let's talk about the $20M ARR moment specifically. Because this is where the growth model changes.

**SAM:** It's a real inflection point. At $5 million ARR, you're grinding new logos. At $10 million, you're starting to build a renewal motion. But at $20 million, the math starts working in your favor in a way it didn't before.

**ALEX:** Walk me through that.

**SAM:** Here's the scenario. You have $20M in ARR. Company A has a net revenue retention of 101% — that's the 2025 median for SaaS companies. Company B has 110% NRR — that's top quartile. Same base, same starting point.

**ALEX:** After one year, Company A has $20.2 million. Company B has $22 million.

**SAM:** Right. But it compounds. After two years, Company A is at $20.4 million. Company B is at $24.2 million.

**ALEX:** That's a $3.8 million gap. Just from expansion.

**SAM:** Before a single new logo. Before a single marketing campaign. Before adding one sales rep. That $3.8 million is what you already earned, from customers you already have. The question is whether you built the machine to capture it.

**ALEX:** And most companies haven't.

**SAM:** The Maxio benchmarks show that B2B SaaS companies are increasingly dependent on expansion ARR — but very few apply the same rigor of process, measurement, or focus to expansion that they apply to new logo acquisition. Expansion has a quota. It usually doesn't have a dedicated motion. It usually doesn't have a rep whose comp actually depends on it.

**ALEX:** And that's a finance problem, not just a sales problem.

**SAM:** Exactly. If expansion isn't instrumented, isn't quota'd, and isn't comp'd — that's a system design failure. And system design is finance's lane.

---

## SECTION 3: THE HUNTER-FARMER-RANCHER MODEL

**ALEX:** Let's get into the mechanics. The hunter-farmer model has been talked about for 20 years. What's actually changed?

**SAM:** A couple things. First, only 15% of SaaS companies actually run a fully bifurcated model. Everyone talks about it, almost nobody does it properly.

**ALEX:** Why not?

**SAM:** Because the handoff is the #1 failure point. Hunter closes a deal, hands it to the farmer, no joint meetings, no documentation, no SLA — and suddenly the customer feels abandoned. Churn goes up. Expansion goes down. Hunter blames farmer. Farmer blames CS. Everyone's confused.

**ALEX:** So what's the fix?

**SAM:** There's a third archetype that's gaining traction in 2025 — the Rancher. Spends about 60% of their time hunting new logos, 40% managing and growing existing accounts. And the key is comp design that explicitly rewards both.

**ALEX:** What does that look like?

**SAM:** Pure hunters: 50/50 base-variable split, 20% accelerator past quota. You want urgency. Half their income depends on closing.

Farmers: 70/30 base-variable, retention bonus when they hold accounts above 90% renewal. You want them stable, long-horizon, relationship-focused.

Ranchers: 60/40, with 15% commission on new business and 8% on expansion. The ratio is intentional — you want them hunting, but with real skin in the game for growth.

**ALEX:** And the case studies actually back this up?

**SAM:** There's one from Predictable Revenue that I keep coming back to. An enterprise sales team had a $20M book of business — same size as our scenario. They shifted to a pure expansion focus. Upsells, cross-sells, renewals. Two years later: $35M.

**ALEX:** Without adding new accounts.

**SAM:** Without adding new accounts. That's 75% growth from customers they already had. The comp plan made it happen. Before the redesign, those AEs were hunting because hunting paid more.

**ALEX:** Behavior follows the money.

**SAM:** Every single time. And if your finance leader isn't designing that comp structure in partnership with the CRO, someone's leaving money on the table.

---

## SECTION 4: PRICING AS THE FINANCE-LED GTM LEVER

**ALEX:** Let's talk about pricing. Because this is where I think finance can make the most immediate impact — and it's usually the most neglected.

**SAM:** The number that should be tattooed on every SaaS CFO's forehead: a 1% improvement in pricing generates 8 to 11% improvement in operating profits.

**ALEX:** That's the highest-leverage improvement in the business.

**SAM:** Higher than a 1% improvement in COGS. Higher than a 1% improvement in S&M efficiency. Higher than a 1% volume increase. Pricing wins by 3 to 7x in operating profit impact.

**ALEX:** And yet most SaaS companies set pricing once when they launch, adjust it maybe every 2 years, and treat it as a sales or product problem.

**SAM:** 78% of SaaS companies now use value-based pricing — that's up from 62% in 2023. That's progress. But the execution is where it breaks down. Value-based pricing without a pricing committee is just a philosophy.

**ALEX:** What does the pricing committee actually look like?

**SAM:** Monthly meeting. Finance leads. RevOps brings the data. CS brings the expansion friction points. One senior sales rep brings the objections they're hearing in the field. Agenda: what's the discounting pattern this month, where are deals dying on packaging, what's the competitive signal, and what are we testing this quarter?

**ALEX:** One test per quarter.

**SAM:** That's the discipline. You don't need a massive pricing overhaul — you need systematic, incremental improvement. New tier. New expansion trigger. Outcome-based language added to the enterprise contract. One thing per quarter, measured, and iterated.

**ALEX:** For property management vertical SaaS specifically, is there anything specific about the pricing model to think about?

**SAM:** Yes, and it's structural. The property management industry prices by unit — per door managed. That's the natural unit of value. And per-unit pricing is one of the most expansion-friendly models in SaaS.

**ALEX:** Because as the customer grows their portfolio...

**SAM:** Their Opiniion bill goes up automatically. No renegotiation needed. No sales call needed. Just growth. That's what you want in an expansion model. The question is whether the per-unit tiers are calibrated to capture that value optimally — or whether there's pricing leakage at specific portfolio sizes.

**ALEX:** That's a pricing audit question.

**SAM:** Which should happen every quarter. With Finance in the room.

---

## SECTION 5: THE CAUSAL MEASUREMENT SYSTEM

**ALEX:** Let's close with measurement. Because everything we've talked about — expansion motion, comp design, pricing — requires the right data to run.

**SAM:** This is the part that most finance leaders skip. They want the levers, not the infrastructure. But the infrastructure is the moat.

**ALEX:** What does causal measurement actually mean in practice?

**SAM:** Let me give you the contrast. Correlation-based measurement — which is what 95% of SaaS companies use — tells you that when a prospect downloads a whitepaper, they're 30% more likely to close. Or that LinkedIn ads correlate with pipeline. That's useful. But it doesn't tell you whether the whitepaper caused the close, or whether it just happened to occur before the close.

**ALEX:** And the distinction matters when you're allocating a $2M marketing budget.

**SAM:** Enormously. Causal measurement — sometimes called marketing mix modeling, or triangulated attribution — goes further. It asks: if I had not run that campaign, what would have happened? It isolates the incremental impact of each investment, controlling for everything else.

**ALEX:** That sounds expensive.

**SAM:** It's less expensive than spending $2M on campaigns that don't work and not knowing it. And the data architecture to support it — connecting HubSpot pipeline to closed-won to cohort-level NRR in NetSuite — is something RevOps can build incrementally.

**ALEX:** So what's the first step?

**SAM:** Honestly? It's declaring NRR as your north star metric and instrumenting it by segment. Start there. NRR by CS rep. NRR by ACV tier. NRR by PM platform integration. Once you can see where expansion is happening and where it isn't — you know exactly where to put the comp dollar, the pricing change, and the CS investment.

**ALEX:** Simple. Not easy.

**SAM:** That's every good finance strategy.

---

## CLOSE

**ALEX:** Let's wrap with the contrarian take. Because we've painted a picture where finance taking GTM control is all upside. What's the risk?

**SAM:** The risk is real. There's good research — from Forma.ai, from Forrester — that when RevOps reports to finance, compensation plans start aligning to cost savings rather than rep performance. The function shifts from growth-enabling to cost-controlling. And you end up with lower S&M spend, tighter budgets, and declining top-line growth.

**ALEX:** Finance as the villain.

**SAM:** Finance as the villain. Which is not the role you want. The unlock is finance as the architect of the game — designing the comp structures, measurement systems, and pricing frameworks — and then getting out of the way of execution. Not running sales. Not cutting headcount. Designing the system that makes the team perform.

**ALEX:** Architect, not auditor.

**SAM:** That's the whole thing. The companies where finance is winning at GTM aren't the ones where the CFO is in every sales call. They're the ones where the CFO built an expansion comp model that made the team's behavior change — and then watched NRR go from 101% to 110% without touching a single deal.

**ALEX:** Alright. Three things to do Monday morning.

**SAM:** Pull your NRR — total and by segment. Find out if your AEs have separate expansion quotas or if they're hunting to make rent. And book 30 minutes to sketch out what a pricing committee would look like for your company.

**ALEX:** That's the playbook. Finance-Led GTM at $20M ARR.

**SAM:** See you next week.
