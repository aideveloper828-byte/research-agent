# Podcast Script: "The $2.65 Million Problem Every PM Company Is Ignoring"
**Date:** April 18, 2026  
**Topic:** Saturday Deep Dive — CFO ROI Model for Property Management Buyers  
**Estimated runtime:** 10–12 minutes (~1,500 words)  
**Hosts:** Alex (finance-focused) and Jamie (proptech/operations-focused)

## COLD OPEN

**Alex:** Let me ask you something. If I told you that the average thousand-unit apartment portfolio is burning through two and a half million dollars a year on a single, largely preventable cost — and that most property management CFOs have no line item for it in their P&L — what would you guess it is?

**Jamie:** Insurance? We've been talking about that forever. Up 55% since 2021, I know the stat.

**Alex:** Good guess. No. It's turnover. Resident turnover. And I'm not talking about just the cost to repaint and replace the carpet. I'm talking about the full all-in hit: make-ready costs, marketing, leasing commissions, and — the one that kills most operators — 40 days of lost rent on every single vacancy.

**Jamie:** 40 days. That's a new number for me.

**Alex:** That's the February 2026 average from Leasey.AI's research — the longest February vacancy fill time since 2019. And when you run the math on a 1,000-unit portfolio at the national average rent of $1,741 a month... you get $2.65 million a year. Every year. And it's getting worse.

**Jamie:** Okay. So what's the solution you're building to?

**Alex:** We're going to build the actual CFO-facing business case for a resident satisfaction platform. Not the marketing pitch. The model. Dollar in, dollar out, payback in months — the kind of thing a finance leader can put in front of their board and defend. Let's go.

## SEGMENT 1: THE COST STRUCTURE NOBODY TALKS ABOUT

**Jamie:** Walk me through the math before we get to the solution. Because I think a lot of operators underestimate how bad the turnover problem actually is.

**Alex:** Okay. Start with the renewal rate. Industry average right now, according to Buildium's 2026 State of Property Management report, is 58%. The industry *target* is 63%. So right out of the gate, the average operator is already 5 percentage points behind where they want to be.

**Jamie:** Five points sounds small.

**Alex:** It's not. On a thousand units, a 42% annual turnover rate means 420 units turn over every year. Four hundred and twenty make-readies. Four hundred and twenty new leases to fill. Let's price one turnover. The 2024 Resident Experience Management Report pegs it at about $4,000 per unit — labor, materials, marketing. Renew, a multifamily analytics firm, puts it at $3,976. Pretty consistent.

**Jamie:** Call it four grand.

**Alex:** Call it four grand. Four thousand times 420 is $1.68 million just in make-ready and marketing. But then add the vacancy. Every unit sits empty for an average of 40 days right now — again, that's February 2026 data, and vacancy fill times are *lengthening*, not shortening. At $1,741 a month, 40 days of lost rent is $2,321 per vacancy. Times 420 turnovers.

**Jamie:** That's another $975,000.

**Alex:** Right. Add it together: $1.68 million plus $975,000 is $2.655 million a year. And here's the kicker — the NAA's 2024 Income and Expense survey shows operators separately reporting $1,323 per unit per year in vacancy and rent loss, plus $82 per unit in concessions, which is up almost 31% year over year. So not only is turnover expensive, the cost of filling vacant units is getting more expensive.

**Jamie:** So we're looking at something like 13% of revenue going to turnover on an average portfolio.

**Alex:** 13.4%, to be precise. That is the largest single controllable NOI drain that most CFOs don't have its own line item for. They see insurance. They see payroll. They see maintenance. They don't see "cost of not retaining residents" as a budget category. That's the gap.

---

## SEGMENT 2: THE RETENTION GAP AND WHY IT EXISTS

**Jamie:** Let's talk about the 5-point gap. 58% actual, 63% target. Why aren't operators closing it?

**Alex:** This is where it gets interesting. And honestly where Opiniion's product story is most powerful. The Buildium report found that property managers think residents leave because of "life changes" — job relocation, buying a home, family situations. Things outside the operator's control.

**Jamie:** That sounds reasonable.

**Alex:** Except it's wrong. When you ask residents directly, the top reasons are: expensive rent, poor maintenance responsiveness, and security concerns. Three things that are entirely within the operator's control. The managers are misattributing churn, and because of that, they're not intervening. They're not making the call, not sending the maintenance crew, not fixing the thing that's actually making someone decide to leave.

**Jamie:** So they're flying blind.

**Alex:** Completely blind. And that's what a resident feedback platform fixes. Opiniion captures sentiment at five moments: tours, move-ins, maintenance requests, lease renewals, and move-outs. You get a signal at every critical point in the resident lifecycle. If someone submits a maintenance request and gives it a 2 out of 5, and then three weeks later doesn't respond to the renewal outreach — that's a churn signal you can act on. Today, most operators don't have that signal.

**Jamie:** What does the research say about the link between feedback data and renewals?

**Alex:** Grace Hill, which runs the Kingsley Index — the largest resident satisfaction database in the industry, over seven million residents surveyed annually — says the link between satisfaction scores and renewal rates is, quote, "undeniable." Buildium's data is more specific: 40% of at-risk renters say they would stay another year if their property manager invested more in maintaining the property. 40%. That's almost half your churned residents who are telling you exactly what would have kept them.

## SEGMENT 3: THE ROI MODEL — BUILDING THE CFO CASE

**Jamie:** Okay, so let's build the model. What does a finance leader need to see to approve this?

**Alex:** Three things. Avoided cost, payback period, and a proof point they can cite. Let's do avoided cost first.

Scenario one: conservative. The platform helps a 1,000-unit portfolio improve renewal rate from 58% to 60%. Two percentage points. That's 20 fewer turnovers a year. At $4,000 make-ready per turn, that's $80,000 saved. Plus 20 units not sitting empty for 40 days — that's another $46,000 in avoided vacancy loss. Total: **$126,000 a year**.

**Jamie:** And scenario two?

**Alex:** Moderate. 58% to 61%. Thirty fewer turnovers. $120,000 in make-ready savings. $70,000 in avoided vacancy loss. **$190,000 total**. That's the number I'd anchor to in a CFO meeting because it's defensible and it's not heroic.

**Jamie:** Optimistic?

**Alex:** If you hit the industry target — 58% to 63% — 50 fewer turnovers, $316,000 in avoided cost per year. For a 2,000-unit portfolio that's over $600,000.

**Jamie:** Now the payback. What does the platform cost?

**Alex:** Opiniion doesn't publish pricing. But resident feedback and survey tools in this vertical generally run $15 to $50 per unit per year. At $30 per unit — $30,000 for a thousand units — the moderate scenario gives you:

Monthly benefit: $190,000 divided by 12 equals about $15,800 a month. Payback period: $30,000 divided by $15,800. **Under two months.**

**Jamie:** Two months.

**Alex:** One turnover costs $4,000 plus $2,321 in vacancy loss — call it $6,300 all-in. So you're paying for the entire year's Opiniion subscription with fewer than five prevented turnovers. That's the line you use in the CFO meeting.

**Jamie:** "We cost less than five prevented turnovers" is actually a really clean anchor.

**Alex:** And there's a secondary value driver that's harder to model but very real. Opiniion also generates Google reviews. Properties with better online ratings fill vacancies faster. If you reduce average vacancy fill time from 40 to 35 days on the units that still turn, that's another $116,000 a year in avoided vacancy loss on a 1,000-unit portfolio. Not modeled in the base case — pure upside.

## SEGMENT 4: THE BUYING PROCESS — HOW TO WIN THE CFO

**Jamie:** Okay, so we have the model. How does the sales motion change?

**Alex:** The insight from B2B procurement research is that deals stall when the CFO enters the conversation at contract time with no prior context. They see a number with no justification and they kill it. The fix is to arm the champion with a model early — before procurement gets involved.

**Jamie:** What does "arming the champion" look like?

**Alex:** Concretely: personalize the model. In discovery, ask "What's your current renewal rate? What's your average make-ready cost?" Plug their numbers in, not industry averages. Then give them a one-page PDF they can forward to their CFO before the demo even happens.

The one-liner I'd put at the top of that PDF: *"We cost less than 8 vacant days. Every renewal we prevent costs nothing — and we're capturing the data that tells you why residents leave before they decide to go."*

**Jamie:** That's good. Finance leaders are trained to think in payback periods and avoided costs. If you can reframe the whole pitch as "this costs you nothing after the first two months," that's a very different conversation.

**Alex:** Exactly. And the proof point: Opiniion ran a case study across 582 communities using both Opiniion and Yardi over an 18-month period. That's not a pilot. That's a dataset. A CFO who wants to validate the ROI claim has a primary source to look at, not just industry benchmarks.

## CLOSE: THE CONTRARIAN TAKE

**Jamie:** Alright, what's the contrarian finding from this research? The thing that cuts against the obvious narrative?

**Alex:** The obvious narrative is "resident satisfaction software pays for itself." And it does. But the contrarian point is this: the ROI model only works if operators *act on the data*. Grace Hill's PerformanceHQ platform has AI-powered analytics that turns survey data into action plans. Buildium's Lumina has human-oversight-first agentic tasks. The value isn't in collecting the feedback — it's in what you do with it in the next 48 hours.

If Opiniion's customers are sitting on a dashboard with a red satisfaction score and doing nothing with it, the renewal rate doesn't move. The CFO case only holds if there's a closed loop: data → intervention → retention outcome. That's the operational discipline question Opiniion should be asking in every quarterly business review.

**Jamie:** So the ROI model is real, but it requires operators to actually use the product.

**Alex:** That's the most important thing a finance leader can say when evaluating any SaaS tool: "Show me your activation and usage data, not just your case studies." A platform with 40% seat utilization doesn't deliver the promised ROI. A platform where every site manager is checking feedback before the weekly stand-up does.

**Jamie:** Good pushback. Alright — tomorrow is Sunday synthesis, but before that: the model is built, it's real, and it's one of the most compelling financial narratives a vertical SaaS company in the PM space can tell. $2.65 million problem, sub-two-month payback, CFO-defensible in a single page.

**Alex:** Build the calculator. Commission the retention study. Price anchor around turnovers, not seat counts. Those are the three moves. See you Sunday.
