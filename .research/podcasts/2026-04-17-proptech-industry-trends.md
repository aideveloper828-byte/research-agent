# PropTech & Property Management Industry Trends
**Podcast Script — Friday Briefing**  
**Date:** April 17, 2026  
**Runtime:** ~10–12 minutes (~1,500 words)  
**Hosts:** Alex (analyst) & Jordan (finance/ops lead)

---

## COLD OPEN

**Alex:** Let's talk about a number that should be stuck in your head right now: 58%.

**Jordan:** That's the percentage of property management companies using AI as of early 2026. And before you say "okay, so what" — here's the context. Two years ago, that number was barely in the double digits. We just crossed a genuine inflection point.

**Alex:** And more importantly, the question in the industry has completely shifted. It used to be "should we adopt AI?" Now it's "which AI features are actually saving us money, and which are just demos that looked good in a sales call?"

**Jordan:** We're going to dig into that today. But we're also going to zoom out — because the macro environment in property management right now is genuinely fascinating. You've got a market where rent growth is basically flat, insurance costs have risen 55% in four years, and the platforms your customers live on are either getting acquired or building features that look suspiciously like what you sell.

**Alex:** It's the Friday PropTech briefing. Let's get into it.

---

## SEGMENT 1: The AI Reality Check

**Jordan:** Okay, so Buildium just dropped their 2026 State of the Property Management Industry Report, and I want to pull out a few numbers that I think tell the real story — not the marketing story.

**Alex:** Hit me.

**Jordan:** First: 58% of PM companies use some AI. But look at what they're actually using it *for*. AppFolio released data showing their Realm-X users are saving an average of 10.3 hours per week on task management and 11.9 hours per week on communications. That's not incremental — that's equivalent to a quarter of an FTE.

**Alex:** And AppFolio is the only vendor rated for "Autonomous Task Execution" in G2's fall 2025 property management grid. Eighty percent score. Their Leasing Performer literally responds to leads and schedules showings at 2am without anyone touching a keyboard.

**Jordan:** Meanwhile Buildium is taking a different approach with Lumina AI — they're explicitly designing for human oversight. The pitch is: we automate the routine stuff, but anything sensitive stays in human hands. Smart positioning, honestly. It acknowledges that a lot of PM operators are not ready to let AI make decisions about residents.

**Alex:** But here's where it gets strategically interesting for anyone selling into this space — and I'll be direct about who I mean: Opiniion. All of these platforms are deepening their AI capabilities. AppFolio, Buildium, Yardi, Entrata. They're all ingesting more resident data. The question becomes: where does a specialized tool like Opiniion's resident feedback platform sit in that ecosystem? Is it a data source these AI systems need? Or is it something these platforms are going to build themselves?

**Jordan:** And that's not rhetorical. That's a board-level strategic question in 2026.

**Alex:** The other AI story I want to flag: fraud. 75% of property management companies reported an increase in rental fraud over the past year. That's one of those numbers that sounds alarming and is alarming. Synthetic identity fraud, fake pay stubs, AI-generated documentation. The industry is in an arms race here.

**Jordan:** And the platforms that nail fraud detection — which now exceeds 98% accuracy on the best tools — are going to have a major NRR tailwind. Because once you integrate your fraud detection into your PM workflow, you are not ripping it out.

---

## SEGMENT 2: The NOI Squeeze — Your Customers Are Under Real Pressure

**Alex:** Let's talk about the cost structure of property management in 2026, because this context matters enormously for understanding why PM companies buy software and how they make buying decisions.

**Jordan:** So: insurance. National multifamily property insurance costs per unit went from $502 in 2021 to $777 in 2024. That's a 55% increase in three years. Houston is over $1,200 per unit. This is not a rounding error — this is a structural NOI hit that every operator is managing.

**Alex:** And at the same time, maintenance costs. Materials are still 30% above pre-pandemic baselines. An additional 8% increase is projected in 2026. 94% of contractors can't fill open positions, which means labor costs are getting bid up. And Buildium's report confirms that 93% of PM companies saw expenses increase over the past year.

**Jordan:** So you've got a customer who is getting squeezed from every direction. Revenue is flat — rent growth at 0.2% nationally, basically zero. Costs are rising across insurance, maintenance, labor, and compliance. What does a finance leader at a PM company do with that?

**Alex:** They cut or they automate. And the Buildium data is pretty explicit: half of PM companies say technology adoption is their *top* strategy for cutting costs. Not renegotiating vendor contracts. Not layoffs. Technology.

**Jordan:** This is actually a very favorable environment for the right software vendor. If you can show a PM company's CFO — not just their ops manager, their *CFO* — that your tool reduces their cost structure in a quantifiable way, you are in a completely different conversation than a tool that just "improves resident satisfaction."

**Alex:** Because here's the math. Turning a unit costs somewhere between $3,000 and $5,000 all-in — cleaning, make-ready, vacancy loss, leasing fees. If you're running a 1,000-unit portfolio and you improve renewal rates by just 1%, you're saving $30,000 to $50,000 per year. That's not a soft benefit. That's a hard number that a CFO can put in a model.

**Jordan:** And who does that? Who improves renewal rates? The platforms that actually capture resident sentiment early enough to act on it.

---

## SEGMENT 3: The Retention Gap — And Why Managers Have It Wrong

**Alex:** Here's the most interesting finding from the research this week, and I want to spend a minute on it. The industry targeted a 63% resident retention rate last year. They achieved 58%. Five points short. 75% of managers expect to improve in 2026.

**Jordan:** But the reason this is so interesting is the *why*. When you ask property managers why residents don't renew, they say: life changes, buying a home, relocating. Classic deflection. And when you ask residents why they *actually* didn't renew — expensive rent, poor maintenance, security concerns.

**Alex:** This is a massive misalignment. Managers think they're losing residents to circumstances beyond their control. Residents are telling researchers they would have stayed with better service. Buildium's report found that 40% of at-risk renters would commit to another year if their PM invested more in property maintenance.

**Jordan:** Forty percent. These are residents who are choosing to leave who would have stayed. That is a solvable problem. It's not fate. It's a service delivery problem that shows up in feedback data — if you're capturing it.

**Alex:** And that feedback window is tight. You don't find out a resident is dissatisfied with maintenance when they hand in their notice. You find out 60 to 90 days before that, when they're submitting tickets and not getting responses. Or when their move-in experience was rocky. Those signals exist. Most PM companies just aren't capturing them systematically.

**Jordan:** This is why the resident experience software market is real and growing. The question is which platforms own the data relationship with the resident.

---

## SEGMENT 4: Market Structure — Consolidation, Pressure, and Where Opiniion Sits

**Alex:** Let me give you some market context and then we'll close with a contrarian take.

**Jordan:** Go for it.

**Alex:** PropTech M&A: 163 deals in the first eleven months of 2025. Full-year 2024 was 134 deals. So we're already well past last year's pace, and the consolidation trend is accelerating. The big platforms — AppFolio, Yardi, MRI, Entrata — are all acquiring point solutions and building out unified platforms.

**Jordan:** The property management software market itself is valued at $3.6 billion in 2025, growing to $5.9 billion by 2033. That's a 6.4% CAGR. Solid, not explosive. But within that, vertical SaaS is outperforming — growing at 16 to 22% CAGR versus 12 to 15% for horizontal. And the retention data is striking: vertical SaaS delivers three times higher customer retention than horizontal competitors.

**Alex:** Because when you're embedded in a specific workflow — tenant screening, lease management, resident feedback — switching costs compound. You're not just replacing software. You're re-training staff, migrating data, breaking integrations.

**Jordan:** The Sunbelt market is worth calling out separately. Vacancy rates in Sunbelt markets are running about 200 basis points higher than coastal and Midwest markets. Austin, Phoenix, Denver — these operators are hurting. They have the most to gain from retention improvement tools. They should be priority targets.

---

## CLOSE — CONTRARIAN TAKE

**Alex:** Okay, contrarian take of the week. Ready?

**Jordan:** Hit me.

**Alex:** Everyone is talking about AI in property management as a productivity tool. Time saved, tasks automated, FTE reduction. And that's real. But I think the more important story — the one that gets underplayed — is AI as a *data asset*.

**Jordan:** Meaning?

**Alex:** The platforms that are deploying AI are training models on proprietary operational data. AppFolio's Realm-X is getting better every month because it's processing millions of lease interactions, maintenance tickets, and resident messages. That data moat is going to become incredibly difficult to replicate.

**Jordan:** So the platform that captures the resident feedback relationship now is building a dataset that will be worth much more later.

**Alex:** Exactly. The question isn't just "does our AI feature save time today?" The question is "who owns the resident data that makes tomorrow's AI better?" And that's a strategic question that most PM companies — and the software vendors selling to them — haven't fully grappled with yet.

**Jordan:** It's Friday. Go think about that over the weekend.

**Alex:** Full brief is linked in the morning email. See you Monday.
