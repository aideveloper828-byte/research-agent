## SEGMENT 1: The Accountability Moment — 2026 Is When Finance Teams Own AI

**ALEX:** So here's the framing I want to start with. If you talk to anyone in finance software right now — FloQast, Datarails, anyone — they'll tell you the same thing: 2025 was the year AI *entered* the accounting workflow. 2026 is the year teams are expected to *own* it.

**JORDAN:** And FloQast surveyed 515 full-time finance and accounting professionals across 12 countries. The finding? The teams actually getting results from AI aren't just "adopting" it — they're collaborating with it. There's a real difference. Adoption is: you have the tool. Collaboration is: you've redesigned the workflow around it.

**ALEX:** Here's the stat that tells the whole story. Gartner found that 78% of CFOs are actively investing in AI. But only 47% believe their teams are equipped to use it effectively. That's a 31-point gap between buying and deploying.

**JORDAN:** And that gap is where the low impact lives. You buy Datarails or Mosaic or Drivetrain, you integrate it, and then... your data is a mess. Your processes weren't designed for automation. You're basically putting a Ferrari engine in a car with no transmission.

**ALEX:** That 67% stat is the one I keep coming back to. In a survey of 150 CFOs whose AI implementations underperformed — 67% said the primary cause was deploying AI before addressing data quality issues. Not the wrong tool. Wrong sequence.

**JORDAN:** So rule number one for the new Finance Director listening to this: your first month is a data quality audit, not a tool selection. What data do you have? Where does it live? How stale is it? How many manual handoffs exist between your ERP, your CRM, your billing system, your HRIS?

**ALEX:** Map the plumbing before you turn on the water.

## SEGMENT 2: The Big Bet — Datarails Raises $70M and Declares War on Legacy FP&A

**JORDAN:** Okay, the biggest news in finance AI this quarter: Datarails raised $70 million in January 2026. Series C, led by One Peak. They've now raised $175 million total. And they grew 70% year-over-year in 2025.

**ALEX:** And they didn't just raise money. They launched FinanceOS — which is a bold product repositioning. The pitch is: we're not just your FP&A tool. We're the operating system for your entire CFO's office.

**JORDAN:** It connects 400+ data sources — NetSuite, SAP, Salesforce, HRIS, Excel, all of it — and does real-time financial consolidation with complex eliminations, FX adjustments, allocations. And then you drop in AI Finance Agents on top.

**ALEX:** The three agents they launched are called Strategy, Planning, and Reporting. And they're interesting because they're generative — they produce board-ready PowerPoints, PDFs, Excel files. You ask "what's driving profitability changes this year?" and you get a formatted analysis with citations, not a raw data dump.

**JORDAN:** What I found most interesting is the pricing model shift. They're moving from per-user SaaS to usage-based. And the CEO Didi Gurfinkel is explicit about why: AI agents, not humans, are increasingly what's driving software consumption. When an AI agent is running 500 analysis queries a night, the per-seat model makes no sense.

**ALEX:** This is actually a really important signal for anyone selling SaaS right now. Datarails is a SaaS company disrupting their own pricing model before someone else does it to them. How many $20M ARR SaaS companies are asking that same question about their own pricing?

**JORDAN:** And for the practical side — Datarails works natively in Excel. That's a major unlock for a finance team that's not going to abandon their models. You don't rip and replace. You layer AI on top of the workflow your team already has.

## SEGMENT 3: The Close — FloQast Agents and the Death of the Month-End Sprint

**ALEX:** Let's talk about the close. FloQast just launched auditable AI Agents — and the word "auditable" is doing a lot of work there. Because the thing finance teams are nervous about with AI is: can I take this to my auditors?

**JORDAN:** FloQast has 2,800 mid-market customers. And their 2026 projection is striking: by the end of this year, the standard month-end close — transaction coding, bank rec, schedule updates, variance analysis — will have "dramatically less human involvement."

**ALEX:** The Continuous Close concept is real now. Instead of a 5-to-7 day sprint at month end, you have reconciliations running daily. Anomalies are flagged in real time. By the time you get to the close, most of it is already done.

**JORDAN:** For a $20M ARR company with a small accounting team, this is transformational. If your close takes five days and FloQast compresses it to two — that's 36 days a year of capacity you just created. You can redirect that to financial analysis, board prep, investor materials, or supporting the GTM team with data.

**ALEX:** And the auditable piece matters for companies in Opiniion's position — if you're building toward a financing event or an acquisition, having clean, traceable, auditable AI-assisted financials is a feature, not a footnote.

## SEGMENT 4: Ramp Is a Platform Now — and the Numbers Are Wild

**JORDAN:** I want to spend a minute on Ramp because people still think of it as a corporate card. It is not a corporate card anymore. It's a finance operations platform that happens to issue cards.

**ALEX:** The numbers: Ramp crossed $1 billion in annualized revenue. $32 billion valuation. And they've deployed four AI agents just in AP: auto-coding, fraud prevention, approval, and payment.

**JORDAN:** The accuracy stat: their Policy Agent reviews 100% of expenses automatically — and it's more than 99% accurate. That's more accurate than human reviewers. So when you run Ramp, you get complete expense policy enforcement without anyone manually reviewing receipts.

**ALEX:** The "zero click" design philosophy is where they're going. They're already at 7x fewer clicks than Bill.com for bill processing. And the goal is literally zero clicks — the AI handles the full AP workflow autonomously.

**JORDAN:** They announced a Visa partnership in 2026 to build AI agents for corporate bill pay. The interesting thing about this is that the controls are built into the transaction itself — not applied retrospectively. That's a fundamental shift in how financial controls work.

**ALEX:** For a Finance Director walking in on day one: if Opiniion isn't already on Ramp, this is probably the fastest ROI available. The implementation is simple. The policy compliance automation is immediate. And the AP efficiency gains are measurable within weeks.

## SEGMENT 5: The $118.5 Billion Problem — Dunning and Collections

**JORDAN:** Here's the contrarian finding — or at least the underappreciated one. Every SaaS finance leader is focused on forecasting and close automation. Almost nobody talks about dunning. But failed payments cost subscription businesses $118.5 billion annually.

**ALEX:** $118.5 billion. Let that sit.

**JORDAN:** Expired cards, bank declines, failed retries. At a $20M ARR company, if 3–5% of monthly charges fail — which is the industry average — and you're recovering 40% of those manually, versus the 76% Smart Retry benchmark that Recurly has published, you have a defined revenue gap you can calculate and own.

**ALEX:** Let's put numbers to it. If you're at $20M ARR — call it $1.67M per month in recurring revenue. 4% fail rate: $67,000 per month in failed charges. At 40% recovery, you're getting $27K back. At 76% recovery, you'd get $51K back. That's $24,000 per month — or about $288,000 per year — of recoverable revenue hiding in your dunning process.

**JORDAN:** And the tools that do this — Maxio, Recurly, Chargebee, even standalone dunning products — use machine learning to optimize retry timing. Not "retry at day 3 and day 7." They analyze payment behavior, predict when a card is most likely to clear, and retry at the optimal moment across optimal channels.

**ALEX:** This is a finance-led revenue impact. It's not a product initiative or a sales initiative. It's the Finance Director running an analysis in week one and surfacing $200–$300K in recoverable ARR. That's the kind of move that establishes credibility fast.

**JORDAN:** And reducing involuntary churn has an LTV multiplier on top of the direct recovery. If you reduce failed-payment churn by 5%, the profit impact — per the research — is 25–95%. Because it's not just the immediate charge. It's the lifetime value of customers you retain.

## CLOSE + CONTRARIAN TAKE

**ALEX:** Okay, so let's bring this home. If you're a Finance Director walking into a $20M ARR SaaS company in April 2026, here's the week-one checklist from today's research:

**JORDAN:** One: don't buy an AI tool yet. Audit your data quality first. Sixty-seven percent of underperforming implementations skipped this step.

**ALEX:** Two: pull your failed payment data. Calculate your current recovery rate versus the 76% benchmark. Quantify the revenue gap. This is fast money and it establishes you as a finance leader who drives revenue, not just reports on it.

**JORDAN:** Three: get demos from Datarails and Drivetrain. Don't commit yet — just understand what your FP&A workflow could look like with AI. Both are targeting the exact ARR range you're at.

**ALEX:** Four: assess Ramp. One meeting, one trial. The >99% expense policy compliance alone might be worth the switch.

**JORDAN:** And five: start building your AI governance position. The FloQast research is clear — teams that just "adopt" AI don't outperform. Teams that redesign workflows around AI-human collaboration do. That means your controller needs to understand what auditable AI means. It means you start the conversation about an AI governance framework before you need it for an audit.

**ALEX:** Now, contrarian take to close. Everything we discussed today is about using AI to make the existing finance function faster. But the *really* interesting question — the one Datarails CEO Didi Gurfinkel is asking himself — is what happens when AI doesn't just speed up your finance function, it replaces the FP&A workflow entirely?

**JORDAN:** He said it directly: "AI can build models and run analysis and create reports much faster and much better than any human." If that's true, what's the finance team's actual job in three years?

**ALEX:** The answer — and this is the bet FloQast's survey supports — is that the job becomes AI collaboration, governance, and strategic interpretation. The CFO who figures that out before their peers will have a permanently defensible advantage.

**JORDAN:** That's the long game. But the short game is: dunning analysis, week one.
