## TL;DR

1. **At $20M ARR, expansion is the game.** Companies in the $15-30M ARR band now generate 40% of new ARR from existing customers — up from 25% in 2022. Most treat expansion as an afterthought. The ones who don't are worth 2-3x more.
2. **CFOs got the GTM mandate because sales couldn't prove causality.** GTM effectiveness has fallen from 78% (2018) to 47% (2025) across 478 B2B companies. When marketing and sales can't show cause-and-effect between spend and revenue, finance fills the void — with cost control. The fix is causal measurement, not more dashboards.
3. **NRR is the single highest-leverage metric you control.** A $20M ARR company at top-quartile NRR (110%) generates $5M more in expansion ARR over 12 months than a peer at median (101%). That's a full quarter of new business, compounding forever.
4. **Hunter-Farmer-Rancher comp design is broken at most companies.** 85% of SaaS companies don't run a properly differentiated model. Without it, behavior follows the money — AEs hunt instead of farm because hunting pays more. One comp redesign at a $20M book of business produced $35M in 2 years without adding headcount.
5. **Pricing is the most underused CFO lever in SaaS.** A 1% price improvement generates 8-11% operating profit. 78% of SaaS companies now use value-based pricing. For vertical SaaS in property management, per-unit pricing structurally outperforms seat-based — it scales with the customer, enables natural expansion, and aligns cost to demonstrated value.

## Key Findings

### 1. The $20M ARR Inflection Point: Expansion Becomes the Engine

Beyond $20M ARR, the growth math changes fundamentally. Expansion revenue is no longer a nice-to-have — it becomes the primary growth driver.

**The data:**
- Companies in the $15-30M ARR band generate 40% of new ARR from existing customers (Maxio 2025 Benchmarks) — up from 25% in 2022, a 60% structural shift in 3 years
- At $50M+ ARR, over 50% of incremental ARR comes from expansion
- Median growth rates have compressed to 26% (Benchmarkit 2025) — the new logo well is getting more expensive to dig
- New CAC has risen 14% while median growth slowed — the efficiency case for expansion has never been stronger

**What the NRR math means at $20M ARR:**
| NRR Scenario | Starting ARR | Year 1 ARR | Year 2 ARR |
|---|---|---|---|
| Top Quartile (110%) | $20M | $22M | $24.2M |
| Median (101%) | $20M | $20.2M | $20.4M |
| Bottom Quartile (95%) | $20M | $19M | $18.1M |

The gap between top and median over 2 years: **$3.8M in organic ARR — before a single new logo.**

**Relevance to Opiniion:** Opiniion sits exactly at this inflection point. The question is not whether to build an expansion machine — it's how quickly. Finance (with RevOps direct reports) is in the optimal org position to architect this.

### 2. CFOs Are Taking Over GTM — And Here's the Structural Reason

The CFO's expanding role in GTM strategy is not a power grab — it's a structural response to a measurement vacuum.

**The core problem (MarTech, Mark Stouse):** "Marketing and sales cannot prove causality. In the absence of causal measurement, CFOs default to cost control and correlation."

**The evidence:**
- GTM effectiveness across 478 B2B companies has fallen from 78% (2018) to 47% (2025) — nearly halved in 7 years
- Win rates are declining, pipeline conversion is decreasing, cost per qualified opportunity has risen significantly
- Traditional correlation-based analytics (last-touch attribution, UTM, MQL counts) have become "actively misleading" in fast-changing environments where historical data can't capture current reality
- Gartner's Q4 2025 CFO survey confirms finance leaders entering 2026 budget cycle are prioritizing "scalable systems over incremental headcount" — a bias toward infrastructure and measurement over people

**The fix:** Causal measurement — distinguishing between what metrics move together and what actually caused what. CFOs with causal models can:
- Make defensible investment decisions ("this channel caused $X in pipeline, through Y mechanism")
- Trace pathways between GTM spend and business outcomes
- Move from cost control to strategic capital stewardship
- Create board-level alignment around shared evidence, not function-level turf wars

**Relevance to Opiniion:** The Head of Finance has 2 RevOps direct reports — an unusual and powerful organizational structure. Most finance leaders get GTM authority because sales failed; this leader can get GTM authority proactively by building the measurement infrastructure that makes GTM legible. The risk: if RevOps stays in CRM-admin mode and Finance stays in budget-cut mode, both functions underperform.

### 3. The Hunter-Farmer-Rancher Model: What Actually Works in 2025

The classic hunter-farmer split sounds clean in theory. In practice, only 15% of SaaS companies run a truly bifurcated model, and most that try fail at the handoff.

**The three archetypes:**

| Role | Focus | Comp Split | Incentive | Failure Mode |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Hunter** | Net-new logos | 50/50 base/variable | 20% accelerator past quota | Ignores expansion, farms easy renewals |
| **Farmer** | Renewals + expansion | 70/30 base/variable | Retention bonus at >90% renewal | Doesn't grow accounts, just protects them |
| **Rancher** | Both (60% new, 40% expand) | 60/40 base/variable | 15% new, 8% expansion | Context-switching fails without process |

**The rancher case study (Predictable Revenue / Mike Entner-Gomez):**
- One enterprise team took a $20M book of business and grew it to $35M in two years — without adding net-new accounts
- Method: pure expansion focus (upsells, cross-sells, renewals) with differentiated comp that rewarded account growth
- Result: 30% YoY growth from a motion most SaaS companies treat as passive

**What kills the split model:**
1. **No handoff SLA** — the #1 failure point. No joint meetings, no documentation = churn risk and stalled expansion
2. **Wrong comp ratios** — if hunters get 20% accelerators but farmers get flat 8% expansion, reps become hunters by default
3. **No pipeline support** — hunters need pre-qualified leads; farmers need customer success backup; ranchers need automation

**Relevance to Opiniion:** Audit current AE comp plans before Q3 quota-setting. Key questions: (1) Are AEs separately quota'd on expansion vs. new logo? (2) Does expansion comp match or exceed new-logo comp per dollar? (3) Is there a formal handoff SLA from onboarding to expansion? Without these, the $20M→expansion-dominant model won't activate.

### 4. Pricing as Finance-Led GTM Lever

Pricing is the most underleveraged strategic tool in SaaS finance — and the one most clearly in finance's lane.

**The ROI math:**
- A 1% improvement in pricing generates 8-11% improvement in operating profits (Metronome 2025)
- By contrast: a 1% improvement in variable costs = 1.5% profit; a 1% increase in volume = 3% profit — pricing wins by 3-7x
- 78% of SaaS companies now use value-based pricing (up from 62% in 2023)
- Outcome-based pricing is being explored by 47% of SaaS companies; only 9% fully implemented

**Pricing trends most relevant to vertical SaaS:**
- **Seat-based → unit-based:** Property management SaaS pricing per door (per managed unit) structurally outperforms seat-based because it scales with the customer's portfolio, not their headcount
- **Credit/consumption models:** 79 companies in the PricingSaaS 500 now offer credit models (up 126% YoY) — useful for survey/feedback tools where usage varies by campaign
- **Hybrid tiers with outcome anchors:** Leading vertical SaaS players are adding outcome-based tiers (e.g., "guaranteed improvement in X or money back") to penetrate CFO-controlled buying processes

**The pricing committee model:**
Finance-led organizations increasingly run a monthly pricing committee: Finance + RevOps + CS + Sales lead. Agenda: review deal patterns (discounting, expansion triggers, packaging objections), benchmark against comps, evaluate new tier options. Result: pricing stays current, expansion opportunities get captured at contract stage, and finance owns the value narrative.

**Relevance to Opiniion:** Two immediate questions: (1) Is Opiniion's current per-unit pricing model maximizing expansion as customers grow their portfolio? (2) Are there packages/tiers that could capture more value from enterprise PM companies vs. SMB? A pricing audit with Finance leading should happen in Q2.

### 5. RevOps + Finance Alignment: The Org Design That Creates or Destroys GTM

RevOps reporting to CFO is a growing structural trend — but it carries a specific risk that most finance leaders don't see coming.

**The risk (Forma.ai/Forrester 2025):** "When RevOps reports to finance, sales compensation plans end up aligned to cost savings rather than rep performance. The function shifts from growth-enabling to cost-controlling."

**The correct design:**
- Finance sets the rules of the game: quota design, comp philosophy, GTM ROI thresholds, NRR targets
- RevOps runs the connective tissue: CRM architecture, deal desk process, pipeline data quality, usage metering, billing-to-finance data flow
- CRO controls execution: territory design, rep management, forecast ownership
- Finance approves GTM investment using causal measurement, not correlation

**What RevOps under Finance should own:**
- Contract lifecycle management and expansion trigger logic
- Usage data pipeline (especially critical for outcome-based or usage-based pricing)
- Deal review process and discounting guardrails
- GTM data architecture connecting HubSpot → billing → NetSuite
- Commission calculation accuracy and audit readiness

**Relevance to Opiniion:** The 2 RevOps direct reports create leverage IF structured correctly. Immediate priority: establish clear charters. RevOps should own measurement infrastructure and expansion process — not just HubSpot admin. Finance should set the "rules of the game" (NRR target, expansion comp ratios, GTM ROI thresholds) and then get out of the way of execution.
